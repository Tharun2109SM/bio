🔬 What are Carbohydrates?

Carbohydrates are molecules made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, usually in the ratio (CH₂O)ₙ. They serve two main roles in life: energy storage (like glucose and starch) and structural support (like cellulose in plant cell walls). Think of them as life's batteries AND building bricks.

🧩 The Three Types
Monosaccharides
Single sugar units — the simplest form. Examples: Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), Fructose, Galactose. They have a carbonyl (C=O) and multiple hydroxyl (–OH) groups. Glucose is the most important — it's the #1 fuel for cells.
Disaccharides
Two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic linkage (formed via dehydration — water is removed). Examples: Sucrose (glucose + fructose) = table sugar; Lactose (glucose + galactose) = milk sugar; Maltose (glucose + glucose) = malt sugar used in brewing beer.
Polysaccharides
Hundreds to thousands of monosaccharides chained together. Can be for storage (starch, glycogen) or structure (cellulose, chitin). The exact function depends on which sugar units are used and HOW they're linked.

🌲 The α vs β Difference — Why It Matters!

Glucose can form two ring shapes: α-glucose (–OH below the ring at C1) and β-glucose (–OH above the ring). This tiny difference causes HUGE consequences:

  • ▸Starch is made of α-glucose → molecules coil into a helix → can be digested by animals (our enzymes break α-linkages).
  • ▸Cellulose is made of β-glucose → every alternate unit is flipped upside-down → the chain is straight → molecules pack into rigid microfibrils → incredibly strong!
  • ▸Animals cannot digest cellulose — we lack enzymes for β-linkages. Cows and termites rely on gut microbes to do it for them.
  • ▸Cellulose is the most abundant organic compound on Earth (~10¹⁴ kg produced per year by plants).
⭐
Exam Tips Remember: Starch = α-glucose = helical = digestible by humans. Cellulose = β-glucose = straight = NOT digestible. Glycogen = animal starch = more branched than amylopectin. Sucrose = glucose + fructose (table sugar). Lactose = glucose + galactose (milk sugar). Dehydration reaction makes glycosidic bonds; hydrolysis breaks them.

💡 Why Proteins are Special

Proteins make up more than 50% of the dry mass of most cells. The name comes from the Greek proteios meaning "first" — because they are truly primary. They speed up chemical reactions, defend against pathogens, transport molecules, provide structural support, enable movement, and carry signals. Everything your body does involves proteins.

🧪 Building Blocks: Amino Acids

There are 20 different amino acids. All have the same backbone: a central α-carbon bonded to an amino group (–NH₂), a carboxyl group (–COOH), a hydrogen, and a unique R group (side chain). The R group is what makes each amino acid different — it can be nonpolar, polar, positively charged, negatively charged, or aromatic.

Nonpolar / Aliphatic
Glycine, Alanine, Proline, Valine, Leucine, Isoleucine, Methionine — hydrophobic; found in protein cores
Polar Uncharged
Serine, Threonine, Cysteine, Asparagine, Glutamine — can form hydrogen bonds
Charged
Positive: Lysine, Arginine, Histidine
Negative: Aspartate, Glutamate — form ionic bonds
Aromatic

📐 The Four Levels of Protein Structure
  • 1°Primary Structure — The unique sequence (order) of amino acids in the chain, linked by peptide bonds. This is like the specific order of letters in a word. Change one amino acid and the whole protein can malfunction (like sickle cell anemia!). Chains <30 aa = peptide; longer = polypeptide.
  • 2°Secondary Structure — Local folding patterns caused by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms (not R groups). Two types: α-helix (coil, hydrogen bond between every 4th amino acid) and β-pleated sheet (two strands lying side by side, connected by H-bonds). Also: turns (sharp U-bends, often containing Glycine or Proline) and loops.
  • 3°Tertiary Structure — The overall 3D shape of the whole polypeptide, determined by interactions between R groups: hydrophobic interactions (nonpolar R groups cluster inside), van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, and disulfide bridges (S–S bonds between two cysteine residues — covalent, very strong).
  • 4°Quaternary Structure — When a protein has multiple polypeptide subunits that come together. Examples: Collagen (3 helices → triple helix → tendons/ligaments/skin); Hemoglobin (2α + 2β subunits, each with an iron-containing heme group that binds O₂).

⚡ What is an Enzyme?

Enzymes are the most remarkable specialized proteins in nature. They act as biological catalysts — they speed up chemical reactions dramatically without being used up in the process. The word "enzyme" comes from the Latin word Zyma meaning "yeast" (Edward Buchner, Nobel Prize 1907, showed fermentation works with yeast juice, not whole yeast).

Key properties: extraordinary specificity for their substrates, work in aqueous conditions at mild temperature and pH, and lower the activation energy of a reaction without changing which reactions are thermodynamically favorable.

🎯 Activation Energy — The Key Concept

Every chemical reaction has an energy barrier called activation energy (Ea) — the minimum energy needed to start the reaction. Even if the overall reaction releases energy (is thermodynamically favorable), reactants still need to be "pushed" over this initial hill by reaching the transition state.

  • ▸Enzymes do NOT change whether a reaction is energetically favorable (ΔG° stays the same).
  • ▸Enzymes DO lower the activation energy barrier → reactions happen much faster.
  • ▸Enzymes stabilize the transition state — the unstable intermediate between reactants and products.
  • ▸Enzymes prevent unwanted side reactions from occurring.
  • ▸Rate-limiting step = the speed at which substrate molecules diffuse into the active site.
🔬 Case Study: Triose Phosphate Isomerase (TPI)

TPI is present in almost ALL organisms — it's nearly a perfect enzyme. It converts Glyceraldehyde 3-Phosphate (G3P) into Dihydroxyacetone Phosphate (DHAP). Both are triose sugars (3-carbon sugars with a phosphate group).

The Problem Without Enzyme
Converting G3P → DHAP is thermodynamically favorable (ΔG° = −1.86 Kcal/mol), BUT the intermediate is cis-enediol (ΔG° = +26 Kcal/mol) — extremely unstable and quickly loses its phosphate group, becoming toxic methylglyoxal.
What TPI Does
TPI stabilizes the cis-enediol transition state. A loop closes over the active site, preventing water entry and stopping the phosphate from floating away. Without this loop, the enzyme is 100,000× slower.
TPI is a Dimer
It's a 250 amino acid polypeptide chain, present as a dimer (two subunits). Only a few amino acids in the active site are responsible for catalysis.

The loop is critical — removing it kills speed by 100,000×. Transition state = cis-enediol = unstable; enzyme stabilizes it. Replacing a key amino acid with wrong type = enzyme broken or slower.

💧 Why Water is Extraordinary

Life on Earth began in water and evolved there for 3 billion years before moving to land. Water's extraordinary properties all stem from one source: the polar covalent O–H bonds in each molecule, which cause hydrogen bonding between molecules.

🔑 Key Properties
  • ▸Hydrogen Bonding: Each water molecule can form H-bonds with up to 4 neighbours. Bonds form, break, and re-form constantly (each lasts ~trillionths of a second). At any instant, all molecules are H-bonded.
  • ▸Cohesion: Water molecules stick to each other via H-bonds → holds water column together in plant vessels → trees can pull water 100m+ upward.
  • ▸Adhesion: Water sticks to other surfaces (e.g., cell walls) via H-bonds → helps counteract gravity in plants.
  • ▸High Specific Heat (1 cal/g·°C): Water absorbs a LOT of heat before its temperature rises → oceans and lakes buffer temperature extremes → stable environment for marine life → coastal areas have milder climates.
  • ▸Ice Floats: Water is less dense as solid than liquid (expands on freezing) → ice floats → insulates liquid water below → aquatic life survives winter.
  • ▸Universal Solvent: Polar water dissolves ionic and polar substances (hydrophilic) → almost all biochemical reactions occur in aqueous solution.
⭐
Exam Tips All water properties stem from polar covalent bonds → H-bonding. High specific heat = temperature stabilizer. Cohesion = water-water bonds. Adhesion = water-other surface bonds. Ice floating = water unique (most solids sink). Water is the universal solvent. Mars research focuses on water because life needs it.

🫧 What are Lipids?

Lipids are hydrophobic molecules — they repel water. This is because they are mostly made of nonpolar C–H bonds (hydrocarbon regions). Even if they have some polar oxygen-containing bonds, the nonpolar parts dominate. They include fats, oils, waxes, and phospholipids.

⚡ Fats: Energy Storage

A fat = glycerol + 3 fatty acids joined by ester linkages (dehydration reaction between –OH and –COOH groups). Called triacylglycerol. Fatty acids have 16–18 carbon skeletons. The nonpolar C–H bonds make fats hydrophobic.

TypeStructureState at Room TempExample
Saturated FatNo C=C double bonds; max H atomsSolid (molecules pack tightly)Butter, lard

🔵 Phospholipids: The Membrane Builders

A phospholipid = glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate group (instead of 3rd fatty acid). The phosphate group (often with a small charged molecule attached) creates a hydrophilic head. The two fatty acid tails are hydrophobic. This makes phospholipids amphipathic — one end likes water, one end hates water.

  • ▸In water, phospholipids spontaneously self-assemble into bilayers (double layers) — hydrophobic tails face inward, hydrophilic heads face outward toward water.
  • ▸This bilayer is the cell membrane — it separates the inside of the cell from the outside world. Cells cannot exist without phospholipids.
  • ▸The inside of the bilayer is oily/nonpolar → most polar and ionic molecules CANNOT cross freely → gives membrane selective permeability.
⭐
Exam Tips Fat = glycerol + 3 fatty acids (triacylglycerol). Phospholipid = glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate (amphipathic). Saturated = solid (butter); Unsaturated = liquid (oil) due to cis double bond kinks. Phospholipid bilayer = cell membrane. Hydrophilic heads OUT, hydrophobic tails IN. "Amphipathic" = has both hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts.
