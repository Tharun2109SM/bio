Chapter 01
Introduction & Bioinspiration
🎯

This course exists because of a powerful idea: nature is the best engineer. Over billions of years of evolution, living organisms have solved engineering problems — energy storage, structural strength, signal processing, self-repair, miniaturization — far more elegantly than humans have in mere centuries of technology. The purpose of studying biology as an engineer is not to become a biologist, but to think like nature.

Why Engineers Must Study Living Things

Living things have several extraordinary properties that no human-made machine has fully replicated:

  • 01Self-improvement through evolution: Organisms continuously improve their designs in response to environmental pressures. No car improves itself over time.
  • 02Sensing and responding: Organisms sense signals (heat, light, chemicals) and produce calibrated responses — think of how the pupil adjusts to light automatically.
  • 03Self-repair: A broken bone heals. A cut skin regenerates. No metal or plastic does this naturally.
  • 04Information storage and transfer: DNA stores all the instructions to build an organism in a molecule smaller than a human hair's width.
  • 05Molecular diversity from simplicity: At the molecular level, all life on Earth uses the same core molecules (DNA, proteins, sugars) — yet the diversity of life is staggering. A tiny change in organization makes all the difference.
💡
The Engineering Analogy
Think of nature as having run a 3.8 billion year R&D program, with selection pressure as the quality control. Every organism that survived is a working prototype. Evolution = "survival of the fittest" = the best-designed organisms reproduce. Bioinspiration means: reverse-engineer what evolution figured out. Examples: Velcro (from burr seeds), sonar (from bats), lotus-effect coatings (from lotus leaves).
⭐
Exam Tip
If asked "Why should engineers study biology?" — the answer covers 5 points: evolution (self-improvement), sensing/responding, self-repair, information storage/transfer, and molecular diversity. The course aims to understand the logical principles of biology from an engineering perspective.
Chapter 02
Elements of Life & Their Bonding
⚛️

Every living thing on Earth is made of atoms. But not all atoms equally. Life has selected a very specific set of elements — and for good reason. Understanding which elements and why gives you the foundation for all of biochemistry.

Atomic Composition of the Cell

A typical living cell has the following elemental composition by number of atoms:

H = 63% // Hydrogen — most abundant
O = 24% // Oxygen
C = 10% // Carbon — the backbone
N = 1.4% // Nitrogen — in proteins & DNA
P = 0.2% // Phosphorus — in DNA, ATP
S = <0.1% // Sulfur — in some amino acids
// Trace: Ca, Cl, K, Na, Mg, Mn, Fe, Se, I...
🔬
Key Fact
C, N, O, H together make up more than 95% of all atoms in living organisms. These are also the lightest elements in the periodic table that can form stable, diverse molecules. As you go down the periodic table, elements get heavier — life chose lightweight for efficiency, just like an engineer chooses lightweight materials for a moving machine.
Why Carbon is the Backbone of Life

Carbon has a valency of 4 — it has 4 unpaired electrons in its outer shell and can form 4 covalent bonds simultaneously. This gives carbon extraordinary versatility:

  • ▸Carbon can bond with itself to form long chains, rings, branches — the backbone of all organic molecules.
  • ▸It can form single, double, and triple bonds (C–C, C=C, C≡C) — each with different properties.
  • ▸No element lighter than carbon in the periodic table has comparable bonding ability. Silicon (below carbon in the same group) can also form 4 bonds, but silicon-based molecules are far less versatile and heavier.
  • ▸The engineering analogy: if you're building a moving machine (like a car), you want the lightest material that can hold complex structures. Carbon is that material for life.

Definition: Valency
Valency is the number of unpaired electrons in the outer orbital shell of an atom. These unpaired electrons are available to form bonds with other atoms. Think of it as the number of "hands" an atom has to grab other atoms. Carbon has 4 hands → can make incredibly diverse structures.

Chapter 02b
Types of Bonds & Interactions in Biological Systems
🔗

How atoms stick together determines everything about how molecules behave. In biological systems, multiple types of bonds and interactions coexist — some strong and permanent, some weak and fleeting. Together they create the complexity of life. Understanding each bond type deeply is essential for understanding proteins, DNA, membranes, and enzymes.

1. Covalent Bonds

A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share a pair of valence electrons. These are the strongest bonds in biology — they define the primary structure of all molecules.

Bond TypeDescriptionEnergy to BreakExample
C–C single bondOne shared pair of electrons80 Kcal/molAll organic backbone chains
C=C double bondTwo shared pairs>80 Kcal/molUnsaturated fats, ring structures
C≡C triple bondThree shared pairsEven higherAlkynes (less common in biology)
💡
How Strong are Covalent Bonds?
Random thermal energy fluctuations at room temperature = ~0.6 Kcal/mol. A C–C covalent bond = 80 Kcal/mol. That means covalent bonds are over 130× stronger than daily thermal noise. They are essentially permanent under biological conditions — unless something (like an enzyme) actively breaks them.
2. Polar Covalent Bonds & Electronegativity

Not all covalent bonds share electrons equally. Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to attract shared electrons toward itself. When two atoms with different electronegativities share a bond, the electrons spend more time near the more electronegative atom — creating partial charges (δ+ and δ−). This is called a polar covalent bond.

📖
The Polarity Rule
If the electronegativity difference between two bonded atoms is ≥ 0.5 → the bond is POLAR.

Examples:
• C–H bond: C = 2.5, H = 2.1 → difference = 0.4 → NONPOLAR (why hydrocarbon tails of fats are nonpolar)
• C–O bond: C = 2.5, O = 3.5 → difference = 1.0 → POLAR
• O–H bond: O = 3.5, H = 2.1 → difference = 1.4 → STRONGLY POLAR (this is why water is polar)
3. Ionic Bonds

An ionic bond forms when one atom completely transfers an electron to another atom. The atom that loses an electron becomes positively charged (cation); the one that gains becomes negatively charged (anion). Opposite charges attract.

  • ▸Ionic bonds are strong in dry conditions but break easily in water — water molecules surround the ions and shield the charges from each other.
  • ▸Example: NaCl (table salt) — Na⁺ and Cl⁻ are ionically bonded. Add water → bond breaks → Na⁺ and Cl⁻ dissolve separately.
  • ▸In proteins, ionic bonds between charged R groups (e.g., Lysine +, Glutamate −) help stabilize tertiary structure — but these are weak in the aqueous cell environment.
4. Hydrogen Bonds

A hydrogen bond forms when a hydrogen atom that is covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom (O or N) is attracted to another electronegative atom nearby. The H carries a partial positive charge (δ+) and the O or N carries a partial negative charge (δ−).

⚡ Properties
  • Individually very weak (~1–4 Kcal/mol)
  • Form and break rapidly (trillionths of a second)
  • Collectively very strong when repeated many times
  • Directional — work best at specific angles
🎯 Where They Matter
  • Water — all water molecules H-bonded to neighbours
  • DNA — base pairs held by H-bonds (A–T: 2, G–C: 3)
  • Protein secondary structure — α-helix and β-sheet
  • Cellulose microfibrils — parallel chains held by H-bonds
5. Van der Waals Interactions

Even nonpolar molecules experience momentary fluctuations in electron distribution, creating temporary dipoles that induce dipoles in neighboring molecules. These very weak, transient attractions are called van der Waals interactions. They are individually negligible but cumulatively significant when many nonpolar groups pack together — as in the hydrophobic core of proteins.

Bond/InteractionStrengthBroken by Water?Biological Role
CovalentVery Strong (80+ Kcal/mol)NoMolecular backbone; peptide bonds; disulfide bridges
IonicModerate (strong in dry)YesSalt bridges in proteins; stabilize charged R groups
HydrogenWeak (1–4 Kcal/mol each)PartiallyWater, DNA base pairs, protein secondary/tertiary structure

⭐
Exam Tip
Know all 5 bond/interaction types. The polarity rule: difference ≥ 0.5 → polar. C–H = nonpolar (0.4). C–O = polar (1.0). O–H = strongly polar (1.4). Ionic bonds break in water. Hydrogen bonds are individually weak but collectively crucial. Van der Waals = always present between any close atoms. Hydrophobic interaction is caused by water excluding nonpolar groups (they cluster together to minimize surface area).
Chapter 03
Water — The Solvent of Life
💧

Life on Earth began in water and spent the first 3 billion years of its existence entirely in water before colonising land. This is no coincidence. Water has a unique set of properties — all arising from its polar structure — that make it the perfect medium for life. No other molecule does what water does.

The Root of Everything: Polarity

A water molecule (H₂O) has two O–H polar covalent bonds arranged at an angle (~104.5°). Because oxygen is much more electronegative than hydrogen (difference = 1.4), it pulls the shared electrons strongly toward itself, creating:

  • δ−A partial negative charge (δ−) on the oxygen atom
  • δ+A partial positive charge (δ+) on each hydrogen atom

These partial charges allow water molecules to attract each other through hydrogen bonds — the δ+ hydrogen of one molecule attracted to the δ− oxygen of a neighbour. At any instant, every water molecule is hydrogen-bonded to up to 4 neighbours. Bonds form, break, and re-form constantly — each bond lasts only a few trillionths of a second, but there's always a network present.

Property 1: Cohesion & Adhesion

Cohesion = water molecules sticking to each other via hydrogen bonds. Adhesion = water sticking to other surfaces (like cell walls) via hydrogen bonds.

🌳
Real-World Example: Trees
How does a 100-metre-tall tree get water from its roots to its leaves? No pump is strong enough. Answer: cohesion + adhesion + evaporation. Water evaporates from leaf surfaces, pulling the water column upward. Cohesion keeps the water column intact (water molecules don't let go of each other). Adhesion keeps water clinging to the walls of narrow xylem vessels. The tallest trees can lift water over 100 metres — roughly ¼ the height of the Empire State Building.
Property 2: High Specific Heat

Specific heat = the energy needed to raise 1 gram of a substance by 1°C. Water's specific heat = 1 cal/g·°C — unusually high compared to almost any other substance. Why?

Because breaking hydrogen bonds requires absorbing energy. When you add heat to water, much of it goes into disrupting H-bonds rather than increasing molecular speed (temperature). Conversely, when water cools, forming new H-bonds releases energy as heat.

  • ▸Large bodies of water (oceans, lakes) absorb enormous heat during the day/summer and release it slowly at night/winter → temperature stabilization
  • ▸Coastal areas have milder climates than inland areas for this reason
  • ▸Ocean temperatures are stable → stable environment for marine life
  • ▸Water covers 71% of Earth's surface → keeps temperature fluctuations within limits that permit life
Property 3: Ice Floats (Lower Density as Solid)

Water is one of the very few substances that is less dense as a solid than as a liquid. When water freezes, each molecule forms hydrogen bonds with 4 neighbours in a rigid, spacious crystalline lattice. This crystal structure has more space between molecules than liquid water — so ice is less dense and floats.

🧊
Why Floating Ice Matters for Life
If ice sank (as most solids do in their liquids), bodies of water would freeze from the bottom up, eventually freezing solid. Instead, ice forms at the surface and acts as an insulating layer, protecting the liquid water and aquatic life below. Fish, microbes, and other organisms survive winter because of this unique property of water.
Property 4: Water as the Universal Solvent

Water dissolves more substances than any other liquid. This is because its polar molecules can interact with and surround ionic and polar solutes — the partial negative oxygen attracts cations (Na⁺), the partial positive hydrogens attract anions (Cl⁻). The ions become surrounded by a "hydration shell" of water molecules and separate from each other.

Nearly every biochemical reaction in a cell happens in aqueous solution. Water is both the solvent and sometimes a reactant (in hydrolysis reactions). Without water as a solvent, cellular chemistry could not occur.

Water & Life on Other Planets

When scientists search for life elsewhere in the universe, they focus first on planets that might have liquid water. To date, over 200 exoplanets have been found, with evidence of water vapour on some. In our solar system, Mars is the most studied: it has polar ice caps and evidence of ancient liquid water. Subsurface ice was photographed by the Mars Phoenix lander in 2008.

⭐
Exam Tip — All 4 Properties of Water
All properties arise from polarity → H-bonding.
1. Cohesion (water-water) + Adhesion (water-other): enables water transport in plants
2. High specific heat (1 cal/g·°C): stabilizes temperatures; coastal climates; marine life
3. Ice less dense than liquid: ice floats; insulates aquatic life in winter
4. Universal solvent: all biochemical reactions in aqueous solution
Chapter 03b
Lipids & Phospholipids
🫧

Lipids are defined by one property: they are hydrophobic — they don't dissolve in water. This behavior stems from their molecular structure, which is mostly nonpolar C–H bonds. Despite this simple defining feature, lipids are extraordinarily diverse in function: energy storage, membrane building, waterproofing, pigments, and hormones.

Fats: Structure & Energy Storage

A fat molecule (triglyceride or triacylglycerol) consists of glycerol + 3 fatty acids, each joined by an ester linkage formed via a dehydration reaction (a –OH from glycerol reacts with –COOH of fatty acid, releasing water).

📖
Definition: Fatty Acid
A fatty acid is a long carbon skeleton (usually 16–18 carbons) with a carboxyl group (–COOH) at one end. The rest is a hydrocarbon chain (all C–H bonds). The nonpolar nature of C–H bonds (electronegativity difference = 0.4 → nonpolar) is why fats repel water. Water molecules hydrogen bond to each other and literally push the nonpolar fat molecules away.
Saturated vs. Unsaturated Fats

💡
The Kink Analogy
Imagine stacking straight rulers vs. bent rulers. Straight rulers (saturated fats) stack perfectly and pack densely → solid at room temperature (like butter). Bent rulers (unsaturated fats) can't pack as efficiently → remain fluid at room temperature (like olive oil). The "bend" in unsaturated fats is caused by the cis configuration of the C=C double bond.
Phospholipids: Building Cell Membranes

A phospholipid is similar to a fat but with one crucial difference: instead of a 3rd fatty acid, the glycerol's 3rd –OH group is joined to a phosphate group (which carries a negative charge). Small polar or charged molecules can be further attached to the phosphate, creating variety.

💧 Hydrophilic Head
  • The phosphate group + attached polar molecule
  • Negatively charged → attracted to water
  • Points outward in a bilayer (toward aqueous environment)
  • "Water-loving" end
🔥 Hydrophobic Tails
  • Two fatty acid chains
  • Nonpolar C–H bonds → repels water
  • Points inward in a bilayer (away from water)
  • "Water-fearing" end

Phospholipids are amphipathic — meaning they have both a hydrophilic part AND a hydrophobic part. When placed in water, they spontaneously self-assemble into a bilayer: hydrophilic heads on both outer surfaces (in contact with water), hydrophobic tails sandwiched in the middle (away from water).

🔬
The Cell Membrane = Phospholipid Bilayer
Every cell in your body is surrounded by a phospholipid bilayer. This bilayer:
• Forms the boundary between the cell interior and exterior
• Is selectively permeable — small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) pass freely; large or charged molecules cannot cross without protein channels
• Creates the electrochemical gradients needed for nerve signals, ATP synthesis, etc.
• Cells literally could not exist without phospholipids.
⭐
Exam Tip
Fat = glycerol + 3 fatty acids (triacylglycerol). Phospholipid = glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate group. Amphipathic = has both hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts. Bilayer: heads OUT (face water), tails IN (face each other). Saturated = solid (straight chain, packs tight). Unsaturated = liquid (cis double bond kink, can't pack). Self-assembly of phospholipids in water is spontaneous — driven by hydrophobic effect.
Chapter 04
Carbohydrates — Sugars & Their Polymers
🍬

Carbohydrates are one of the four major classes of biological macromolecules. They include simple sugars and large polymers of sugars. Their roles are dual and critical: energy supply (glucose powers cells; starch and glycogen are energy banks) and structural support (cellulose builds plant cell walls; chitin builds insect exoskeletons). The molecular formula of most carbohydrates follows the pattern (CH₂O)ₙ — hence the name "carbo-hydrate" (carbon + water).

Level 1: Monosaccharides — The Single Sugars

Monosaccharides (Greek: monos = single, sacchar = sugar) are the simplest carbohydrates and the monomers from which all larger carbohydrates are built. All monosaccharides have:

  • ▸A carbonyl group (C=O) — either as an aldehyde at the end of the chain (aldose sugars) or a ketone within the chain (ketose sugars)
  • ▸Multiple hydroxyl groups (–OH) attached to the carbon skeleton
  • ▸A molecular formula that is a multiple of CH₂O
Classifying Monosaccharides
CriterionCategory 1Category 2Examples
Carbonyl position Aldose — carbonyl group at the END of the carbon skeleton (forms an aldehyde –CHO) Ketose — carbonyl group WITHIN the carbon skeleton (forms a ketone C=O) Glucose = aldose; Fructose = ketose
Carbon chain length Triose (3C): Glyceraldehyde (aldose), Dihydroxyacetone (ketose)

📖
Glucose — The Central Sugar
Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) is the most important monosaccharide. It is the primary fuel for cellular respiration. In solution, glucose exists mostly as a ring (not a straight chain). The ring forms when the carbonyl group at C1 bonds to the –OH at C5. The position of the –OH at C1 after ring formation creates two forms:
• α-glucose: –OH at C1 is BELOW the ring plane
• β-glucose: –OH at C1 is ABOVE the ring plane
This tiny difference has enormous consequences for polymers made from each form!
Level 2: Disaccharides — Double Sugars

A disaccharide is formed when two monosaccharides are joined by a glycosidic linkage — a covalent bond formed via a dehydration reaction (one –OH from each sugar reacts, releasing a water molecule and forming a bond).

DisaccharideComponentsLinkageWhere FoundCommon Name
MaltoseGlucose + Glucose1→4 α-glycosidicGerminating grains; used in brewing beerMalt sugar
SucroseGlucose + Fructose1→2 α-glycosidicPlants transport carbs as sucrose (leaves → roots)Table sugar

⚠️
Dehydration vs. Hydrolysis
Dehydration reaction (condensation): removes water → BUILDS glycosidic bonds → assembles polymers.
Hydrolysis: adds water → BREAKS glycosidic bonds → breaks polymers back into monomers.
These two reactions are opposites and underlie all polymer assembly and breakdown in biology.
Level 3: Polysaccharides — The Polymers

Polysaccharides are macromolecules — polymers of hundreds to thousands of monosaccharides joined by glycosidic linkages. Their architecture (which sugars, which linkages, branched or unbranched) determines their function entirely.

Storage Polysaccharides
Plants

Starch

Stored in plastids (chloroplasts) as granules. Made of α-glucose monomers joined by 1→4 linkages. Two forms:

Amylose: unbranched, helical coil
Amylopectin: branched at 1→6 points

Released by hydrolysis when glucose is needed. Major dietary sources: potatoes, wheat, corn, rice.

Animals

Glycogen

Stored in liver and muscle cells. Made of α-glucose, like starch, but much more heavily branched than amylopectin (branches every 8–12 glucose units vs every 24–30 in amylopectin).

More branching = more free ends = faster hydrolysis = faster glucose release when energy demands spike.

Structural Polysaccharides
The Critical α vs β Distinction — Starch vs Cellulose

Both starch and cellulose are polymers of glucose. Both use 1→4 glycosidic linkages. Yet one you can eat and digest; the other you cannot. The only difference? α-glucose vs. β-glucose.

🌾 Starch — α-glucose
  • All glucose in α configuration (–OH at C1 below ring)
  • Adjacent units in same orientation
  • 1→4 α-linkages → molecule curves → helical coil
  • Digestible by humans (α-amylase enzyme breaks α-linkages)
  • Function: energy storage
🌿 Cellulose — β-glucose
  • All glucose in β configuration (–OH at C1 above ring)
  • Every alternate unit is flipped 180°
  • 1→4 β-linkages → molecule stays perfectly straight
  • NOT digestible by most animals (no enzyme for β-linkages)
  • Parallel straight chains H-bond together → microfibrils
  • Function: structural strength in plant cell walls
🌍
Cellulose — The Most Abundant Organic Molecule on Earth
Plants produce approximately 10¹⁴ kg (100 billion tons) of cellulose per year. It is the most abundant organic compound on Earth. Cellulose microfibrils (parallel cellulose molecules held by H-bonds, ~80 molecules per microfibril) are grouped into cable-like structures that give plant cell walls incredible tensile strength. Cellulose is also the major constituent of paper and the only component of cotton.
Cellulose as Dietary Fiber

Humans cannot digest cellulose — we lack cellulase enzymes. However, cellulose in food (labeled as "insoluble fiber") still plays an important role:

  • ▸Cellulose abrades the walls of the digestive tract, stimulating mucus secretion that lubricates the passage of food
  • ▸It adds bulk to feces, aiding elimination and preventing constipation
  • ▸Despite not being a nutrient, it is an important part of a healthy diet
  • ▸Fresh fruits, vegetables, and whole grains are rich in cellulose
🐄
Cows, Termites & Gut Microbes
Cows and termites CAN "eat" cellulose — but not because they have cellulase enzymes. They harbor cellulose-digesting microorganisms (prokaryotes and protists) in their digestive systems. The cow/termite provides a home for the microbes; the microbes break cellulose into glucose and other compounds that nourish the host. This is a beautiful example of symbiosis — life relying on other life to extract energy from the most abundant organic molecule on Earth.
⭐
Exam Tip — Master Summary
Formula: (CH₂O)ₙ
Monosaccharides: Aldose (C=O at end) vs Ketose (C=O within). Hexoses = 6C = C₆H₁₂O₆
Disaccharides: Sucrose (glucose+fructose, table sugar), Lactose (glucose+galactose, milk sugar), Maltose (glucose+glucose, beer)
Starch: α-glucose, 1→4 linkages, helical, digestible, energy storage
Glycogen: α-glucose, MORE branched than starch, animal energy storage (liver & muscle)
Cellulose: β-glucose, 1→4 linkages, straight, NOT digestible, structural (plant cell walls)
Key: α vs β = tiny molecular difference → completely different properties
Chapter 05
Proteins & Their Structures
🧬

If carbohydrates are life's fuel and building materials, proteins are life's workforce. They are the most abundant biological macromolecules, occurring in all parts of the cell. The word protein comes from the Greek proteios — meaning "first" or "primary." This is apt: proteins make up more than 50% of the dry mass of most cells and are involved in virtually every biological process.

Proteins range in size from small peptides (a few amino acids) to enormous polymers with molecular weights in the millions. Their functions are staggeringly diverse: enzymes (catalysis), antibodies (immune defense), hemoglobin (transport), collagen (structural support), actin and myosin (movement), hormones (signaling), and receptors (sensing).

The Building Blocks: Amino Acids

All proteins are built from the same 20 amino acids. Every amino acid shares the same core structure but differs in its R group (side chain):

Central α-carbon bonded to:
–NH₂ // amino group (basic)
–COOH // carboxyl group (acidic)
–H // hydrogen
–R // side chain (R group) — unique to each amino acid

The R group determines each amino acid's chemical properties: its size, shape, charge, and whether it is attracted to or repelled by water. Amino acids are linked into chains via peptide bonds — covalent bonds formed between the –COOH of one amino acid and the –NH₂ of the next, with water released (dehydration).

Key Terminology
Peptide: Short chain of amino acids (<20–30) linked by peptide bonds, with a defined sequence
Polypeptide: Longer chain (up to ~4000 amino acid residues)
Protein: A polypeptide (or complex of polypeptides) with a well-defined 3D structure and function
The Four Levels of Protein Structure

Protein structure is organized into four hierarchical levels. Each level builds on the one below it. The final 3D shape of a protein determines its function — change the shape, change or destroy the function.

Primary Structure · 1°
The Amino Acid Sequence

The primary structure is simply the linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, held together by peptide bonds (covalent). This sequence is dictated by the gene encoding the protein.

Why does sequence matter so much? Because the sequence determines how the chain will fold at higher levels. Even a single amino acid change can drastically alter a protein's function. Classic example: sickle cell anemia — one amino acid substitution (Val for Glu at position 6 in hemoglobin β-chain) changes the protein's shape and causes red blood cells to become rigid and sickle-shaped.

⭐
Exam Tip
Primary structure = sequence. The bond = peptide bond (covalent, very strong). Determined by DNA. Any change in sequence = different protein with potentially different function.
Secondary Structure · 2°
Local Folding Patterns: α-Helix & β-Pleated Sheet

Secondary structure arises from hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms — specifically between the C=O (partial negative) and N–H (partial positive) groups of the polypeptide backbone. Note: these are NOT interactions between R groups — the R groups play no role at this level.

α-Helix

The polypeptide backbone coils into a right-handed helix. Each turn of the helix is held by H-bonds between the C=O of one amino acid and the N–H of the amino acid 4 positions further down the chain. The R groups stick outward from the helix. The α-helix is a delicate but stable structure found in many proteins (hemoglobin subunits are primarily α-helical).

β-Pleated Sheet

Two or more segments of the polypeptide chain lie side-by-side (called β-strands) and are connected by H-bonds between their backbones. The chains can run parallel or antiparallel. The structure looks like pleated paper — hence the name. β-sheets are found in structural proteins (silk) and at enzyme active sites.

Turns & Loops

Turns are short (3–4 residues), U-shaped structures on the protein surface that redirect the polypeptide backbone back toward the interior. Stabilized by one H-bond between end residues. Glycine (tiny R group = no steric clash) and Proline (rigid ring structure = built-in bend) are commonly found in turns. Turns allow large proteins to fold into compact globular structures.

Loops are longer, irregular bends with no single well-defined structure. They can take many conformations and are often found on the protein surface where they can interact with ligands or other proteins.

⭐
Exam Tip
Secondary = H-bonds on BACKBONE (not R groups). α-helix = coil (H-bond every 4th amino acid). β-sheet = side-by-side strands (H-bonds between parallel backbones). Turns = U-shaped, Gly + Pro common. Loops = flexible, longer bends.
Tertiary Structure · 3°
Overall 3D Shape of the Polypeptide

Tertiary structure is the overall 3-dimensional shape of the whole polypeptide, resulting from interactions between R groups (side chains) of amino acids that may be far apart in the primary sequence but come close in 3D space.

The Folding Analogy
Imagine the polypeptide as a long, flexible necklace dropped into water. The "oily" (hydrophobic) beads hate water and rush to huddle in the center. The "water-loving" (hydrophilic) beads stay on the outside. This spontaneous rearrangement is the driving force of protein folding. Disulfide bridges are like staples that lock the final shape in place.
⭐
Exam Tip
Tertiary = overall 3D shape. Stabilized by R group interactions: hydrophobic (core), Van der Waals (core), H-bonds (polar R), ionic bonds (charged R), disulfide bridges (Cys–Cys, only COVALENT one). Hydrophobic interaction = most important driving force for folding. Disulfide bridges = strongest (covalent) — found in extracellular proteins.
Quaternary Structure · 4°
Multi-Subunit Assembly

Many functional proteins consist of more than one polypeptide chain. Quaternary structure is the overall protein structure that results from the association of two or more polypeptide subunits, held together by the same types of interactions as tertiary structure (hydrophobic, H-bonds, ionic, disulfide).

Fibrous Protein

Collagen

Three identical polypeptide chains, each in a left-handed helix, intertwined into a right-handed triple helix. This gives collagen fibers extraordinary tensile strength.

Found in: skin, bone, tendons, ligaments, cartilage, blood vessel walls.

Collagen accounts for 40% of the total protein in a human body.

Globular Protein

Hemoglobin

Four polypeptide subunits: 2α subunits + 2β subunits. Each subunit is primarily α-helical in secondary structure.

Each subunit contains a non-protein heme group with an iron atom (Fe²⁺) at its center that binds one O₂ molecule.

So hemoglobin can carry 4 O₂ molecules simultaneously.

⭐
Exam Tip
Quaternary = multiple subunits. Collagen = 3 helical chains → triple helix → structural protein → 40% of body protein. Hemoglobin = 2α + 2β subunits → each has a heme group with iron → binds O₂ → transport protein. Both are classic exam examples of quaternary structure.
Chapter 06
Enzymes — Nature's Perfect Catalysts
⚡

Enzymes are the most remarkable and specialized proteins in existence. They have extraordinary catalytic power — often far greater than any synthetic catalyst — and extreme specificity for their substrates. They operate in aqueous solution at mild temperature and pH conditions. They are at the heart of every metabolic pathway in every living organism.

The word "enzyme" comes from the Latin word Zyma, meaning yeast — because Edward Buchner (Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 1907) showed that fermentation (converting sugars to alcohol) could be done with cell-free yeast juice, proving it was due to chemical molecules, not living cells.

What Enzymes Do — and Don't Do
✅ What Enzymes DO
  • Reduce activation energy (Ea) — the energy barrier that must be overcome for a reaction to proceed
  • Stabilize the transition state — the highest-energy intermediate between reactants and products
  • Prevent side reactions — highly specific, only catalyze the intended reaction
  • Speed up the reaction — dramatically, sometimes by a factor of 10¹⁷
  • Work repeatedly — not consumed in the reaction; can be used over and over
❌ What Enzymes DON'T DO
  • Do NOT change ΔG° — the thermodynamic favorability of a reaction is fixed; enzymes cannot make an unfavorable reaction favorable
  • Do NOT change the equilibrium — they speed up both forward and reverse reactions equally
  • Do NOT get consumed — they are catalysts, not reactants
Activation Energy — The Energy Barrier

Every chemical reaction has an activation energy (Ea) — the minimum energy that reactant molecules must have to begin the chemical transformation. Even if a reaction is energetically favorable (releases free energy overall; ΔG° < 0), the molecules must still be "pushed" over this initial energy hill to reach the transition state — the unstable, highest-energy intermediate that sits at the top of the energy barrier.

Without enzyme:
Reactant → [Transition State: HIGH energy] → Product
Energy barrier = LARGE Ea → reaction is slow

With enzyme:
Reactant → [Enzyme-stabilized Transition State: LOWER energy] → Product
Energy barrier = SMALLER Ea → reaction is fast
ΔG° = same in both cases (thermodynamics unchanged)
💡
The Mountain Pass Analogy
Imagine hiking from a high valley (reactants) to a lower valley (products) — going downhill overall (thermodynamically favorable). But between them is a mountain pass (the transition state). Without a guide (enzyme), you must struggle over the highest peak to get through. With a guide, they show you a lower mountain pass — same start, same end, same overall descent, but the barrier you have to cross is MUCH lower. You get there much faster.
Case Study: Triose Phosphate Isomerase (TPI) — The "Perfect Enzyme"

TPI is present in virtually all living organisms and is considered nearly a perfect enzyme — meaning its rate-limiting step is not the catalytic chemistry itself, but simply how fast substrate molecules diffuse into the active site. Once a substrate is in, the product forms. TPI is a 250 amino acid polypeptide chain that exists as a dimer (two identical subunits).

The Reaction: G3P → DHAP

TPI converts Glyceraldehyde 3-Phosphate (G3P) into Dihydroxyacetone Phosphate (DHAP). Both are triose sugars (3-carbon) with a phosphate group. This reaction is part of glycolysis (the breakdown of glucose for energy).

G3P → DHAP
ΔG° = −1.86 Kcal/mol // Thermodynamically FAVORABLE (product has lower free energy)

BUT the intermediate is cis-enediol:
ΔG° of transition = +26 Kcal/mol // Transition state is HIGHLY unstable
⚠️
The Problem Without TPI
The transition state (cis-enediol intermediate) is extremely unstable. Without an enzyme to stabilize it, the phosphate group spontaneously falls off, converting cis-enediol into a toxic compound called methylglyoxal (not DHAP). The reaction goes wrong. The enzyme is essential not just to speed up the reaction, but to ensure the reaction goes to the right product.
How TPI Works — Step by Step
1
Proton Donation to C1 of G3P
Histidine at position 95 (His95) is the 95th amino acid. It is positively charged and precisely positioned in the active site to donate an H⁺ (proton) to the carbon-1 (C1) of G3P. This initiates the isomerization.
2
Proton Removal from C2 of G3P
Glutamic acid at position 165 (Glu165) is negatively charged and positioned to accept (remove) an H⁺ from carbon-2 (C2) of G3P. Together with His95's donation, this creates the cis-enediol intermediate. The enzyme stabilizes this otherwise highly unstable molecule.
3
Phosphate Stabilization by Lys12
Lysine at position 12 (Lys12) is positively charged. It electrostatically interacts with the negatively charged phosphate group of the substrate, holding it in place. Without this interaction, the phosphate would float away (forming toxic methylglyoxal).
4
Loop Closure — The Critical Guard
Once the substrate binds, a flexible loop region of TPI closes over the active site like a lid. This loop contains 4 polar amino acids that form hydrogen bonds with the cis-enediol intermediate, preventing it from floating away. It also excludes water from the active site — critical because water would facilitate the unwanted methylglyoxal side reaction.
5
Proton Return → Product DHAP Forms
In step 2, Glu165 took H⁺ from C2 and became protonated. Now it donates that H⁺ to C1 of the intermediate. His95 takes H⁺ from C2. The result is DHAP — the correct product. The loop opens, DHAP is released, and the enzyme is ready for the next substrate molecule.

One of the most powerful ways to understand an enzyme is to mutate its key residues and measure what happens. These experiments reveal exactly which amino acids matter and why:

🎯
The "Perfect Enzyme" Concept
TPI is called "perfect" because its catalytic rate is limited only by the rate at which substrate molecules diffuse into the active site — not by the chemistry itself. The chemistry is so perfectly optimized that the enzyme is essentially "waiting" for the next substrate molecule. This is the pinnacle of enzyme evolution: every possible improvement to the chemistry has already been made.
Summary of Enzyme Properties
  • 1Does NOT change ΔG° — thermodynamics is fixed; enzyme cannot make unfavorable reactions favorable
  • 2DOES lower activation energy (Ea) — reduces the energy barrier → reaction is faster
  • 3Stabilizes the transition state — makes the unstable intermediate less unstable
  • 4Prevents side reactions — high specificity ensures only the intended product forms
  • 5Speeds up reaction — dramatically; some enzymes accelerate reactions 10¹⁷-fold
  • 6Not consumed — enzyme is regenerated after each reaction cycle; can be reused
  • 7Works in mild conditions — aqueous solution, physiological temperature and pH

🎮 Practice Quiz
20 Questions · Test your knowledge · Click an option to see if you're right
QUESTION 01
Which property is TRUE about enzymes?
✅ Enzymes lower the activation energy (Ea), not ΔG°. ΔG° (thermodynamic favorability) is a property of the reaction itself and cannot be changed by a catalyst. Enzymes are not consumed — they are regenerated after each cycle.
QUESTION 02
What is the key structural difference between starch and cellulose?
✅ Both starch and cellulose are made of glucose linked by 1→4 glycosidic bonds. The critical difference is the configuration of glucose: starch uses α-glucose (–OH below ring at C1) → helical; cellulose uses β-glucose (–OH above ring at C1) → every unit flipped → straight chains → microfibrils.
QUESTION 03
In Triose Phosphate Isomerase (TPI), what is the role of Lysine at position 12?
✅ Lysine12 is positively charged and positioned to electrostatically interact with the negatively charged phosphate group of the substrate. This holds the substrate in place. Replace it with leucine (no charge) → enzyme completely non-functional. Replace with arginine (+ charge, but bulkier) → 200× slower.
QUESTION 04
Which bond type is responsible for holding the α-helix secondary structure of proteins together?
✅ Secondary structure (α-helix and β-sheet) is maintained by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms — specifically between the C=O (partial negative) and N–H (partial positive) of the polypeptide backbone. R groups are NOT involved at this level.
QUESTION 05
A fatty acid with NO C=C double bonds is called:
✅ A saturated fatty acid has no C=C double bonds — it is "saturated" with hydrogen atoms. All carbons carry the maximum number of H atoms. Saturated fats are solid at room temperature (straight chains pack tightly). Unsaturated fats have one or more C=C double bonds, causing kinks, making them liquid.
QUESTION 06
What causes phospholipids to spontaneously form a bilayer in water?
✅ Phospholipids are amphipathic — they have a hydrophilic phosphate head (loves water) and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails (fear water). In water, the hydrophobic tails cluster together to minimize contact with water, while heads remain in contact with water. This spontaneous self-assembly creates the bilayer — the basis of all cell membranes.
QUESTION 07
Collagen has what type of quaternary structure?
✅ Collagen has three identical helical polypeptide chains intertwined into a right-handed triple helix. This structure gives collagen fibers exceptional tensile strength, making it ideal as a structural component of skin, bone, tendons, and ligaments. Collagen = 40% of total human body protein.
QUESTION 08
Why does ice float on liquid water?
✅ When water freezes, each molecule forms H-bonds with 4 neighbors in a rigid, spacious crystalline lattice. This lattice has more space between molecules than liquid water → ice is less dense than liquid water → ice floats. This is unusual — most solids are denser than their liquids. Ice floating insulates liquid water below, protecting aquatic life in winter.
QUESTION 09
What is the only COVALENT interaction that stabilizes protein tertiary structure (besides the peptide backbone)?
✅ Disulfide bridges (–S–S–) form between the sulfhydryl groups (–SH) of two cysteine residues when they are brought close together during folding. The sulfur atoms bond covalently, creating a very strong link that "rivets" parts of the protein together. All other tertiary structure interactions (H-bonds, ionic, hydrophobic, van der Waals) are non-covalent and relatively weak.
QUESTION 10
Sucrose is composed of:
✅ Sucrose (table sugar) = glucose + fructose. Lactose (milk sugar) = glucose + galactose. Maltose (malt sugar) = glucose + glucose. Plants transport carbohydrates from leaves to roots in the form of sucrose.
QUESTION 11
If you replace Glutamate165 with Aspartate165 in TPI, what happens?
✅ Replacing Glu165 with Asp (same negative charge, but one fewer –CH₂– group → shorter by one carbon) makes TPI about 1,000× slower. This shows that even ONE carbon bond of difference in positioning the critical charge changes efficiency 1000-fold. Position matters as much as charge type!
QUESTION 12
Which polysaccharide is MORE branched than amylopectin?
✅ Glycogen (animal starch) is similar to amylopectin but has even more branching. Amylose is unbranched; amylopectin is branched; glycogen is the most branched. More branching = more free ends = faster hydrolysis = faster glucose release — important for animals that need rapid energy mobilization (e.g., muscle contraction, fight or flight).
QUESTION 13
What is the specific heat of water?
✅ Water's specific heat = 1 cal/g·°C. This is unusually high — water absorbs or releases a large amount of heat per degree of temperature change. This is because much of the heat goes into breaking or forming hydrogen bonds rather than changing molecular kinetic energy (temperature). Random thermal energy at room temperature ≈ 0.6 Kcal/mol.
QUESTION 14
Why does removing the loop in TPI reduce enzyme speed by 100,000×?
✅ The loop closes over the active site after substrate binding. It performs three critical functions: (1) keeps the cis-enediol intermediate in place via H-bonds, (2) excludes water from entering the active site (water would facilitate the side reaction forming methylglyoxal), and (3) ensures the reaction goes to the correct product (DHAP). Without it, the enzyme is 100,000× slower and makes wrong products.
QUESTION 15
What does "amphipathic" mean?
✅ Amphipathic molecules have both a hydrophilic ("water-loving") region and a hydrophobic ("water-fearing") region. Phospholipids are the classic example: the phosphate head is hydrophilic (faces water), the two fatty acid tails are hydrophobic (face away from water). This property drives spontaneous bilayer formation and is the basis of all biological membranes.
QUESTION 16
Which amino acids are commonly found in protein "turns" (U-shaped bends)?
✅ Glycine has the smallest possible R group (just –H), so it creates no steric clash in tight bends. Proline has a ring structure that creates a built-in rigidity and bend in the polypeptide backbone. Together, they allow the chain to fold into the tight U-shaped turn structure. These turns redirect the polypeptide backbone and allow large proteins to fold into compact globular shapes.
QUESTION 17
The electronegativity difference between C and H is 0.4. This means the C–H bond is:
✅ The rule: electronegativity difference ≥ 0.5 → polar bond. C–H difference = 0.4 → NONPOLAR. This is why hydrocarbon regions of fatty acids are hydrophobic — C–H bonds share electrons almost equally, creating no partial charges, so water (which is polar) cannot interact with them. This is the molecular basis of fat being hydrophobic.
QUESTION 18
Hemoglobin consists of how many polypeptide subunits, and how many O₂ molecules can it carry?
✅ Hemoglobin has 4 polypeptide subunits: 2α + 2β. Each subunit contains a non-protein heme group with an iron (Fe²⁺) atom at its center. Each iron atom can bind one O₂ molecule. So hemoglobin carries 4 O₂ molecules simultaneously. Both α and β subunits are primarily α-helical in secondary structure.
QUESTION 19
Cellulose cannot be digested by humans because:
✅ Cellulose uses β-glycosidic linkages. Human digestive enzymes (like α-amylase) can only break α-linkages (as in starch). The distinctly different 3D shape of cellulose's β-linkages prevents our enzymes from fitting and hydrolyzing them. Cows and termites can digest cellulose only because they harbor gut microbes that produce cellulase enzymes.
QUESTION 20
What percentage of dry mass in a cell does protein account for?
✅ Proteins account for ~50% of the dry mass of most cells — by far the largest fraction. The remaining dry mass: ~15% carbohydrates, ~15% nucleic acids, ~10% lipids/fats. (Note: 63% is the percentage of atoms in a cell that are hydrogen — a different metric entirely.)
